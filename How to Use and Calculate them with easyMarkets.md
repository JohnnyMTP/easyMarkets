# How to use and calculate pivot points

**Pivot Points** are commonly used in technical analysis to highlight potential support and resistance levels. These horizontal reference lines help traders anticipate where the price may reverse, consolidate, or accelerate. They are frequently applied in short-term approaches, including [intraday trading.](https://www.easy-markets.com/eu/learn-centre/discover-trading/what-is-a-day-trade/)

Many charting platforms calculate pivot points automatically, but they can also be determined manually using data from the previous trading session.

---

## What pivot points represent

Pivot Points are calculated **support and resistance levels** based on the previous session's high, low, and closing prices. Traders use them to:

- Assess overall market sentiment, with price above the pivot often viewed as bullish and below the pivot as bearish
- Identify areas where price may reverse
- Improve the timing of trade entries and exits

They are widely used in forex, futures, and stock index trading

## Calculating pivot point levels

Pivot levels are derived from three key values from the previous trading session:

- High price
- Low price
- Close price

Several calculation methods exist, but the most widely used is the **five-point system**. This method produces:

- 1 Pivot Point (P)
- 2 Resistance levels (R1, R2)
- 2 Support levels (S1, S2)

The formulas are as follows:

P = (High + Low + Close) / 3 \
R1 = (2 × P) – Low \
R2 = P + (High – Low) \
S1 = (2 × P) – High \
S2 = P – (High – Low)

Where:

**High** = The highest price from the previous session \
**Low** = The lowest price from the previous session \
**Close** = The closing price from the previous session

These five levels serve as **reference points** for the following trading day.

The Pivot Point, R1, and S1 are generally considered the most significant levels. R2 and S2 can also be calculated, resulting in five horizontal lines on the chart: R1, R2, Pivot Point, S1, and S2.

## Plotting pivot points on a chart

In **markets that operate 24 hours a day, such as forex,** the high, low, and close are often based on the **New York closing time of 5:00 PM EST.** To plot pivot points:

- Review the **previous day's** [**candlestick**](https://www.easy-markets.com/au/learn-centre/understanding-analysis/identifying-candlestick-patterns-and-momentum/)
- Record the **high, low, and close values**
- Apply these values to the formulas
- Mark the five calculated levels on the chart

This approach can also be applied to **shorter timeframes**, such as 1-hour or 5-minute charts, depending on the trading strategy being used.

## Example of a pivot point calculation

Assume trading takes place on January 15, using the data from January 14:

- High = 1.1659
- Low = 1.1633
- Close = 1.1648

Applying the formulas:

- P = (1.1659 + 1.1633 + 1.1648) / 3 = 1.1647
- R1 = (2 × 1.1647) – 1.1633 = 1.1660
- R2 = 1.1647 + (1.1659 – 1.1633) = 1.1673
- S1 = (2 × 1.1647) – 1.1659 = 1.1634
- S2 = 1.1647 – (1.1659 – 1.1633) = 1.1621

The five resulting levels to be plotted on the chart would be:

R2: 1.1673 \
R1: 1.1660 \
Pivot: 1.1647 \
S1: 1.1634 \
S2: 1.1621

![How to plot pivot points](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/d1a0f342-472b-44f5-a908-220d22ef1d61)

As with any technical indicator, pivot points do not provide 100% guaranteed outcomes and may not always predict price movements accurately. Even so, many traders include them on their charts as potential areas where price may react during the trading session, regardless of whether pivot points form the core of their strategy.

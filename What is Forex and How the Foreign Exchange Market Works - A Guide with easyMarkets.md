# What is Forex and How the Foreign Exchange Market Works: A Guide with easyMarkets

**Forex**, short for **foreign exchange**, is the global marketplace where currencies are exchanged. It is considered the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with more than 7.5 trillion dollars traded daily. Unlike stock or commodity markets, forex operates 24 hours a day, five days a week, across major financial centres such as London, New York, and Tokyo.

---

## Understanding Forex trading

Forex trading involves **buying one currency while selling another at the same time**. Currencies are always quoted in **pairs**, for example **EUR/USD**, which represents the euro against the US dollar. Exchange rates change continuously due to factors such as economic data releases, interest rate decisions, political developments, and overall market sentiment. Traders attempt to benefit from changes in these exchange rates.

For instance, if a trader expects the euro to strengthen against the US dollar, they may choose to **buy** EUR/USD. If they expect the euro to weaken, they may choose to **sell** the pair.

## What is a currency pair?

Every forex transaction involves a currency pair, which expresses the value of one currency relative to another. A currency pair consists of two parts:

- A **base currency**, which appears first
- A **quote currency**, which appears second

Using **EUR/USD** as an example:

- **EUR** is the base currency
- **USD** is the quote currency

If the exchange rate is **1.09705**, this indicates that **1.09705 US dollars** are required to purchase **1 euro.**

## How Forex trading works

When viewing a currency pair, two prices are typically displayed:

- **Ask price**: The price paid when buying the currency pair
- **Bid price**: The price received when selling the currency pair

The difference between the ask and bid prices is known as the **spread**. In forex markets, this difference is generally small.

## Categories of currency pairs

Currency pairs are commonly grouped into three categories based on their trading volume: major pairs, minor pairs, and exotic pairs.

### Major currency pairs

Major pairs are the most actively traded and liquid currency pairs. Each of them includes the **US dollar.** They often have tighter spreads and relatively high volatility.

Examples include:

- **EUR/USD** (Euro / US Dollar)
- **GBP/USD** (British Pound / US Dollar)
- **AUD/USD** (Australian Dollar / US Dollar)
- **USD/JPY** (US Dollar / Japanese Yen)
- **USD/CHF** (US Dollar / Swiss Franc)
- **NZD/USD** (New Zealand Dollar / US Dollar)
- **USD/CAD** (US Dollar / Canadian Dollar)

### Minor currency pairs

Minor pairs, also known as cross pairs, do not include the US dollar. Although they may be slightly less liquid than major pairs, they remain widely traded and often reflect regional economic relationships.

Examples include:

- **GBP/JPY** (British Pound / Japanese Yen)
- **EUR/GBP** (Euro / British Pound)

### Exotic currency pairs

Exotic pairs consist of one major currency paired with a currency from a **smaller or emerging economy.** These pairs may experience larger price movements, but they typically come with wider spreads and higher risk.

Examples include:

- **USD/MXN** (US Dollar / Mexican Peso)
- **USD/ZAR** (US Dollar / South African Rand)
- **USD/SGD** (US Dollar / Singapore Dollar)
- **USD/HKD** (US Dollar / Hong Kong Dollar)
- **USD/DKK** (US Dollar / Danish Krone)
- **USD/SEK** (US Dollar / Swedish Krona)
- **USD/NOK** (US Dollar / Norwegian Krone)
